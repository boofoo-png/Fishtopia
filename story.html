Introduction

The story begins when Finn receives a formal letter from the bank explaining that their grandfather has passed away and that his estate will be seized if it is not claimed in person. Finn never had a relationship with their grandfather and has no strong memories of him other than some photos they've seen. While Finn is confused, there is something uncomfortable about letting a piece of family history disappear without even seeing it. The letter contained a map to a small island, Purradise Island, where the estate is located. After some hesitation, Finn decides to go.

Finn uses a small boat and follows the thin scratches on the old map. The trip is quiet, giving Finn way too much time to think about their grandfather and why he chose to live so far away after Finn was born. As the island comes into view, a storm rolls in suddenly. The water becomes rough, with crashing waves, and the boat strikes rocks near the shore. Finn loses control and is thrown into the sea. They wake up later on the beach, soaked and disoriented, with their boat beyond simple repair.

Main Quest

The main quest of Fishtopia is to 'escape the island', except there is no time-limit, deadline or evil monster racing you to the end, you are meant to play at your own pace, and as you do, the player character discovers maybe it isnt so bad on Purradise Island after all.

In order to rebuild your boat you will need to help re-establish the fishing infrastructure of Purradise Island by catching and selling fish to the local general store and cataloguing them in your journal.

Conclusion

Although Finn arrives intending to settle paperwork and leave, the island begins to feel more complicated. More alluring. There are old journals and areas of the island that carry unspoken tension and mystery. Finn may not have known their grandfather in life, but by living in his shoes, they begin to understand that his decision to stay on the island was not accidental. Over time, the island begins to feel less temporary. What began as an obligation slowly becomes a question of belonging.
